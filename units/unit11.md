## Contract Law Basics

### What is a Contract?

**Contract**: Legally enforceable agreement between competent parties

**Requirements for Enforceability**:
1. Legal purpose
2. Offer and acceptance
3. Competent parties
4. Consideration
5. In writing (for real estate under Statute of Frauds)

---

## Essential Elements of Valid Contract

### 1. Competent Parties

**Legally capable of contracting**:
- Legal age (18+ in most states)
- Sound mind
- Not under duress or undue influence

**Incompetent Parties**:
- Minors (can void contract)
- Mentally incompetent
- Intoxicated persons
- Under duress or fraud

### 2. Mutual Consent (Offer and Acceptance)

**Offer**: Proposal to enter into contract
- Must be definite and certain
- Communicated to offeree
- Can be withdrawn before acceptance

**Acceptance**: Agreement to exact terms of offer
- Must be unqualified (mirror image rule)
- Communicated to offeror
- Creates binding contract

**Counterofferr**: Changes to original offer
- Rejects original offer
- Creates new offer
- Original offer is dead

### 3. Lawful Objective

**Contract purpose must be legal**:
- Cannot enforce illegal contracts
- Examples of illegal purposes:
  - Discrimination
  - Fraud
  - Restraint of trade

### 4. Consideration

**Something of value exchanged**:
- Can be money, property, services, promise
- Must have legal value
- Need not be equal value (adequate consideration)

**Examples**:
- Buyer's payment
- Seller's property
- Promise to perform
- "Good and valuable consideration"

### 5. In Writing (Statute of Frauds)

**Real estate contracts must be in writing**:
- Purchase agreements
- Leases over one year
- Listing agreements
- Options

**Exceptions** (oral may be valid):
- Leases under one year (most states)
- Fully executed contracts
- Part performance may enforce

## Classification of Contracts

### By Formation:

**Express Contract**:
- Stated in words (oral or written)
- Terms clearly articulated
- Most real estate contracts

**Implied Contract**:
- Created by actions and conduct
- Not stated in words
- Example: Accepting services implies you'll pay

### By Execution:

**Executed Contract**:
- All obligations completed
- Fully performed
- Example: After closing, deed recorded, payment made

**Executory Contract**:
- Obligations not yet completed
- Performance still due
- Example: Purchase agreement before closing

### By Parties Bound:

**Bilateral Contract** ⭐ (Most Common):
- **Both parties make promises**
- Promise for a promise
- Both have obligations
- Example: Purchase agreement (buyer promises to pay, seller promises to deliver deed)

**Unilateral Contract**:
- **One party makes promise**
- Other party performs
- Only promisor is bound until performance
- Example: Option contract (seller promises to sell IF buyer exercises option)

### By Validity:

**Valid Contract**:
- Meets all legal requirements
- Enforceable in court

**Void Contract**:
- No legal effect
- Cannot be enforced
- Example: Contract for illegal purpose

**Voidable Contract**:
- Valid but can be disaffirmed by one party
- Example: Contract with minor
- Injured party can void or enforce

**Unenforceable Contract**:
- Valid but cannot be proven in court
- Example: Oral real estate contract (Statute of Frauds)

## Types of Real Estate Contracts

### 1. Purchase Agreement (Sales Contract)

**Most important contract in real estate transaction**

**Also called**:
- Purchase and sale agreement
- Offer to purchase
- Sales contract
- Earnest money contract

**Essential Elements**:
- Parties (buyer and seller)
- Property description
- Purchase price
- Earnest money amount
- Financing terms
- Closing date
- Contingencies
- Signatures of all parties

### 2. Listing Agreement

- Covered in Unit 10
- Employment contract between seller and broker

### 3. Option Contract

**One party has right (option) to buy property**:
- Buyer pays for option
- Seller must sell IF buyer exercises
- Buyer not obligated to buy
- **Unilateral contract**
- Must be exercised within time period

**Components**:
- Option fee (consideration)
- Exercise price
- Expiration date
- Property description

### 4. Land Contract (Contract for Deed)

**Installment sale of property**:
- Seller finances purchase
- Buyer makes payments to seller
- **Seller holds title** until paid off
- Buyer has equitable title
- Buyer takes possession

**Also called**:
- Installment contract
- Contract for deed
- Land sales contract

**Risks**:
- Buyer: Can lose all payments if default
- Seller: Difficult to remove buyer

### 5. Lease Agreement

- Covered in leasing unit
- Contract for property use

### 6. Lease-Purchase Agreement

**Combines lease with option or agreement to purchase**:
- Tenant leases property
- Right or obligation to buy
- Rent credits may apply

## Purchase Agreement Components

### Key Provisions:

**Parties**:
- Full names of buyers and sellers
- Marital status
- How title will be taken

**Property Description**:
- Legal description or street address
- Inclusions (fixtures staying)
- Exclusions (what seller takes)

**Purchase Price and Payment Terms**:
- Total price
- Earnest money deposit
- Down payment amount
- Loan amount and type
- Balance at closing

**Closing**:
- Closing date
- Possession date
- Prorations (taxes, HOA, utilities)
- Who pays closing costs

**Contingencies** (Conditions):
- Financing
- Inspection
- Appraisal
- Sale of buyer's property
- Title review
- Home sale contingency

**Default and Remedies**:
- What happens if buyer defaults
- What happens if seller defaults
- Earnest money disposition
- Liquidated damages

**Disclosures**:
- Property condition
- Lead paint (pre-1978 homes)
- Environmental hazards
- Material defects

**Standard Clauses**:
- Time is of the essence
- Entire agreement clause
- Severability
- Assignment
- Governing law

## Contingencies

**Contingency**: Condition that must be satisfied for contract to be binding

### Common Contingencies:

**1. Financing Contingency**:
- Buyer must obtain loan
- Specifies loan amount, rate, type
- Time period to secure financing
- If loan denied, buyer can cancel

**2. Inspection Contingency**:
- Property must pass inspection
- Buyer can negotiate repairs
- Can cancel if unsatisfactory
- Time period to inspect

**3. Appraisal Contingency**:
- Property must appraise at/above purchase price
- Lender requirement
- Buyer can cancel if low appraisal

**4. Sale of Buyer's Property**:
- Buyer must sell current home first
- Seller risk: Property tied up
- May include "kick-out clause"

**5. Title Contingency**:
- Clear, marketable title required
- Time to review title commitment
- Seller must cure defects

**6. Homeowners Association Review**:
- Review HOA documents
- Financial statements
- CC&Rs and bylaws
- Time period to review and cancel

---

## Earnest Money

**Purpose**:
- Shows buyer's good faith
- Applied to purchase price at closing
- Held in broker's trust account

**Amount**:
- Varies by market and price
- Typically 1-3% of purchase price
- Negotiable

**Disposition**:
- Applied to down payment/closing costs
- Returned to buyer if contract fails (with valid contingency)
- May be forfeited if buyer defaults
- Disputed earnest money held until resolution

---

## Contract Performance and Discharge

### Ways Contract is Discharged:

**1. Performance**:
- Complete fulfillment of obligations
- Closing and deed delivery

**2. Partial Performance**:
- Some obligations completed
- May allow enforcement of oral contract

**3. Agreement of Parties**:
- Mutual rescission
- Both agree to cancel
- Novation (substitute new contract)

**4. Impossibility of Performance**:
- Property destroyed
- Cannot be legally performed
- Death of party (personal service contracts)

**5. Operation of Law**:
- Statute of limitations
- Bankruptcy
- Merger (fulfilled at closing)

---

## Breach of Contract

**Breach**: Failure to perform contract obligations

### Types of Breach:

**Material Breach**: Substantial failure
- Contract cannot be performed
- Innocent party can sue

**Minor Breach**: Technical violation
- Contract substantially performed
- Damages may be reduced

---

## Remedies for Breach

### Seller's Remedies (Buyer Defaults):

**1. Sue for Specific Performance**:
- Force buyer to complete purchase
- Rarely used by seller

**2. Liquidated Damages**:
- Keep earnest money as damages
- Amount specified in contract

**3. Sue for Actual Damages**:
- Compensate for losses
- Difference in sales price if resold lower

### Buyer's Remedies (Seller Defaults):

**1. Sue for Specific Performance**:
- Force seller to sell
- Most common buyer remedy
- Real estate is unique (adequate remedy at law unavailable)

**2. Rescission**:
- Cancel contract
- Return of earnest money

**3. Sue for Damages**:
- Compensate for losses
- Costs incurred

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Confusing bilateral and unilateral contracts
❌ Thinking oral real estate contracts are valid (Statute of Frauds!)
❌ Mixing up void and voidable
❌ Forgetting consideration is required
❌ Thinking counteroffer accepts original offer (it rejects it!)

## Key Comparisons

### Bilateral vs. Unilateral Contract

| Bilateral | Unilateral |
|-----------|-----------|
| Promise for promise | Promise for act |
| Both parties bound | One party bound |
| Example: Purchase agreement | Example: Option contract |
| Most common | Less common |

### Valid, Void, Voidable, Unenforceable

| Type | Characteristics | Example |
|------|----------------|---------|
| Valid | Meets all requirements, enforceable | Properly executed purchase agreement |
| Void | No legal effect, cannot be enforced | Contract for illegal purpose |
| Voidable | Can be disaffirmed by one party | Contract with minor |
| Unenforceable | Valid but cannot be proven | Oral real estate contract |
