## Key Terms

- **Nonagent**: Person who facilitates transaction without representing either party
- **Customer**: Person whom agent assists but does not represent
- **Fiduciary Relationship**: Relationship of trust and confidence
- **Special Agent**: Agent authorized for single transaction
- **Agent**: Person authorized to act on behalf of another
- **General Agent**: Agent with ongoing authority for all business matters
- **Implied Agency**: Agency created unintentionally through actions
- **Law of Agency**: Body of law governing agent-principal relationships
- **Puffing**: Exaggerated statements of opinion
- **Principal**: Person represented by agent

## Agency Relationships

### What is Agency?

**Agency**: Fiduciary relationship where agent represents principal

**Key Parties**:
1. **Principal (Client)**: Person being represented
2. **Agent**: Person representing the principal
3. **Customer**: Third party (not represented)

### Creation of Agency

**Express Agency**: Created by written or oral agreement
- Listing agreement
- Buyer representation agreement
- Property management agreement

**Implied Agency**: Created by actions and conduct
- ⚠️ **Warning**: Can be unintentional!
- Acting as if you represent someone
- Providing services beyond customer service
- Making promises or commitments

**Agency by Ratification**: Principal accepts previously unauthorized acts

---

## Types of Agency Relationships

### By Scope of Authority

**1. Universal Agent**:
- Broadest authority
- Can act in all legal matters
- Example: Power of attorney for all affairs
- Rare in real estate

**2. General Agent**:
- Ongoing authority for all business in specific area
- Example: Property manager
- Multiple transactions
- Continuing relationship

**3. Special Agent** ⭐ (Most Common in Real Estate):
- Limited to single transaction
- Authority ends when transaction completed
- Examples:
  - Listing agent selling property
  - Buyer's agent purchasing property

## Fiduciary Duties

### The Fiduciary Relationship

Agent owes **highest standard of care** to principal (client)

### Six Fiduciary Duties: **"OLD CAR"**

**1. Obedience**
- Follow lawful instructions
- Cannot deviate from client's direction
- Refuse unlawful or unethical instructions

**2. Loyalty**
- Put client's interests first
- No self-dealing
- Disclose any conflicts of interest
- Cannot represent competing interests without consent

**3. Disclosure**
- Inform client of all material facts
- Share information that affects client's decisions
- Disclose own interest in transaction
- Provide market conditions information

**4. Confidentiality**
- Keep client information private
- Continues after agency ends
- Exceptions: Required by law, client consent
- ⚠️ **Cannot disclose**:
  - Client's motivation to sell/buy
  - How much client will accept/pay
  - Client's financial situation
  - Personal information

**5. Accounting (Care of funds)**
- Handle client money properly
- Deposit in trust account
- Provide accurate records
- Account for all funds

**6. Reasonable Care and Skill**
- Professional competence
- Due diligence
- Stay informed about property and market
- Investigate property condition
- Honest and accurate information

## Types of Agency Representation

### 1. Seller Agency (Listing Agent)

**Agent represents**:
- Property seller
- Owes fiduciary duties to seller
- Customer service to buyer

**Responsibilities**:
- Market property effectively
- Obtain best price and terms for seller
- Disclose all offers
- Advise seller on market conditions

### 2. Buyer Agency

**Agent represents**:
- Property buyer
- Owes fiduciary duties to buyer
- Customer service to seller

**Responsibilities**:
- Find suitable properties
- Negotiate best price and terms for buyer
- Advise on property condition
- Recommend inspections

### 3. Dual Agency

**Agent represents both buyer and seller in same transaction**

**Requirements**:
- **Full disclosure** to both parties required
- **Written consent** from both parties
- Some states prohibit dual agency
- Some states allow designated agency

**Challenges**:
- Loyalty conflicts
- Cannot fully represent both
- Limited confidentiality
- Reduced fiduciary duties

**Prohibited in some states** - Check local law!

### 4. Designated Agency

**Solution to dual agency problem** (where allowed):
- Two agents from same brokerage
- One designated to represent seller
- One designated to represent buyer
- Each owes full fiduciary duties to their client
- Broker remains dual agent

### 5. Transaction Broker (Facilitator/Non-Agent)

**Agent represents neither party**:
- Provides services without representation
- No fiduciary duties
- Ministerial acts only
- Honesty and fairness required
- Must disclose material facts

## Duties to Third Parties (Customers)

Even to customers (non-clients), agent owes:

1. **Honesty and fair dealing**
2. **Disclosure of material facts**
   - Property defects known to agent
   - Environmental hazards
3. **No fraud or misrepresentation**
4. **Reasonable care**

**Material Fact**: Information that would affect party's decision
- Must disclose even to customers
- Includes property defects
- Stigmatized property issues (varies by state)

---

## Agency Disclosure

### Disclosure Requirements

**When**: At first substantive contact (varies by state)
- Before confidential information exchanged
- Before showing property (many states)
- In writing

**What to Disclose**:
- Whom agent represents
- Types of agency available
- Agent's duties to each party
- Compensation arrangements

**Agency Disclosure Form**: Required in most states

---

## Termination of Agency

### By Agreement
- Completion of purpose (property sold)
- Expiration of listing period
- Mutual agreement to cancel

### By Operation of Law
- Death of either party
- Bankruptcy of either party
- Destruction of property
- Insanity of either party

### By Acts of Parties
- Revocation by principal (may owe commission)
- Renunciation by agent (may breach contract)

## Special Topics

### Stigmatized Properties

**Stigmatized Property**: Property psychologically impacted by event

**Examples**:
- Death occurred on property
- Reported paranormal activity
- Site of crime

**Disclosure rules**: Vary greatly by state
- Some states prohibit asking/telling
- Some require disclosure
- Check local law

### Puffing vs. Misrepresentation

**Puffing** (Legal):
- Exaggerated opinion
- Salesperson's enthusiasm
- Subjective statements
- Example: "Best house in neighborhood!"

**Misrepresentation** (Illegal):
- False statement of fact
- Can be intentional (fraud) or negligent
- Example: "New roof" when roof is 20 years old

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Confusing client and customer (client = principal being represented)
❌ Creating implied agency accidentally
❌ Disclosing client confidential information
❌ Thinking puffing is illegal (it's legal opinion)
❌ Forgetting to disclose agency relationship
❌ Mixing up general and special agents
❌ Violating confidentiality after agency ends

## Key Comparisons

### Client vs. Customer

| Client (Principal) | Customer (Third Party) |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Represented by agent | Not represented |
| Receives fiduciary duties | Receives honesty and fairness |
| Agency agreement exists | No agency agreement |
| Full loyalty | Impartial treatment |
| All information disclosed | Material facts only |

### Single Agency vs. Dual Agency

| Single Agency | Dual Agency |
|---------------|-------------|
| Represents one party | Represents both parties |
| Full fiduciary duties | Limited fiduciary duties |
| No conflict of interest | Inherent conflict |
| Clear loyalty | Divided loyalty |
| Preferred arrangement | Requires disclosure and consent |

## Critical Warnings

⚠️ **Never disclose client's**:
- Motivation to sell/buy
- How much they'll actually accept/pay
- Urgency to close
- Financial situation
- Personal information

⚠️ **Always disclose**:
- Material facts about property
- Your agency relationship
- Conflicts of interest
- Property defects you know about

⚠️ **Avoid implied agency by**:
- Clarifying who you represent
- Not giving advice beyond customer service
- Providing agency disclosure early
- Not creating expectations of representation
